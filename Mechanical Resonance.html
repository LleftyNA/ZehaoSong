Determination of damping coefficient of an oscillating hacksaw blade from amplitude, phase measurements and transient decay

  • Department of Physics, Simon Fraser University
  • Burnaby, British Columbia Canada V5A 1S6
Introduction

What is mechanical resonance? Do you know that mechanical resonance can be very dangerous? See this video! Mechanical resonance is essential in musical instruments, architecture, and mechanical system design. The damping effect will always influence the oscillatory system, which re- duces oscillation amplitude. The experiment will cover the mechanical resonance of a mass attached to a hacksaw blade in this lab. An accelerometer[1] is connected to the end of the blade, so the motion of the mass can be measured by Labview. A magnet is placed at the end of the blade and uses a bar of copper to add magnetic force to add damping. All analysis code are in python. If you need the code for your project, sorry I can't help you, but I can provide some information. Feel free to contact me.

Abstract

I report here on measurements of the amplitude of oscillation, the phase shift between the input voltage and motion, and transient decay data. I will use different methods to fit the damping coefficient of three other damping cases: minimal, moderate, and high damping. Then, the fitting results are used to calculate quality factor Q, a dimensionless parameter that shows how underdamped an oscillatory system is.

Method and Experiment

The accelerometer will report position in the unit of voltage, which is not a good unit for studying motion. By turning the whole system ±90 deg, the ratio between voltage and gravitational acceleration can be easily found. The result is 0.32V = g. So I can divide my voltage data by a factor of 0.32 to get in the unit of g. The value for g is 9.8m/s2.

Connecting the function generator to a vibrating driver[2] and put the driver’s end on the hacksaw blade near the anchor block(around 2cm). There is some position requirement. If the driver is placed too close to the accelerometer, it will lose contact with the blade. On the other hand, being too close to the anchor block will greatly reduce the effectiveness of the driver. Setting up the voltage of function generator Vpp = 2V , so the amplitude will be around 1V . Then, find the point where the amplitude of oscillation is the largest, which in my case, the resonance frequency is f = 12.9kHz. Next, I measured several points around this frequency to process further analysis. The oscillatory motion is fitted to

$$X(t) = X_{0} + A\cos\left({\frac{ 2\pi t}{T} + \varphi}\right) \ \textbf{,} \label{Eq:1}$$

where X is the position of the mass, X0 is the offset/initial position of the mass, A is the amplitude of the oscillation, T is the period and φ is the phase shift factor. By using the curve fit algorithm in Python, an estimated value for amplitude will be the result. With resonance frequency w0 and damping coefficient γ, the amplitude and frequency follow the relationship

$$|a_{0}(w)| = \frac{\omega^2 \left(\frac{F_{0}}{m} \right)}{\sqrt{\left(\omega_{0}^2 - \omega^2 \right)^2 + 4\gamma ^2\omega^2}} \ \textbf{.} \label{Eq:2}$$

F0/m is assumed to be a constant. By plotting amplitude vs frequency data and curve fit it, I get the minimal damping fitting in Fig. 1. Results are resonance frequency ω0 = 12.92±0.02kHz and damping factor γ = 0.239 ± 0.083. The error is estimated from the standard deviation. As we can see, the curve fits the data well. The resonance frequency is close to 12.9kHz and the damping factor is close to zero, which means there is not a lot of force that causes damping to the system. Next, I repeat the process for moderate damping by moving the copper bar on top of the magnet, covering nearly half of the magnet with the copper bar. Repeating the fitting process for amplitude, then use the fitting result for estimating ω0 and γ. I get the following result, see the middle plot of Fig. 1. The fitting result shows ω0 = 12.96 ± 0.06kHz and damping factor γ = 2.038 ± 0.463, which is still consistent with our resonance frequency. The γ increases reveal that the magnetic force creates some damping effect, which reduces the oscillation. After that, by moving the copper position to cover the whole magnet, the system will receive the largest magnetic force, which will cause the most damping. See the right side plot of Fig. 1. I get ω0 = 12.99 ± 0.12kHz and damping factor γ = 5.190 ± 1.033. The resonance frequency still stays consistent at 12.9kHz, and the damping factor increases a lot. Comparing three damping cases, we can see that from Fig. 1, minimal damping results in the least outer force, so when the frequency is near resonance, the amplitude of oscillation increase significantly. Compared to the first case, moderate damping has a much lower amplitude at the resonance frequency, which reveals damping can affect oscillation crucially. In the high damping case, the amplitude at resonance frequency is the lowest, which means outer force prevents the system from oscillating a lot.

Another way to estimate resonance frequency and the damping factor is to use the relationship between phase shift and frequency by the following equation

$$\theta = \arctan \frac{2\gamma \omega}{\omega^2 - \omega_{0}^2} + \delta \ \textbf{,} \label{Eq:3}$$

where θ is the phase shift angle between the input voltage and oscillation, and δ is the offset of phase shift. The offset may be caused by the voltage shift of the accelerator and DAQ device. So the phase shift value will be θaccel − θinput. By fitting Eq. (1) for both voltages, I get estimate values for phase shift φ and subtract them.

After using Eq. (3) and fit for ω0, γ and δ of minimal damping data, I get results with ω0 = 12.92 ± 0.02kHz, γ = 0.455 ± 0.132 and δ = −0.29 ± 0.09. Repeating the phase shift fitting method for moderate and high damping data, I get moderate damping data fitting and high damping data fitting(in Fig. 2). From the fitting result, all ω0 values are in the range of 12.9kHz, and γ increases with the damping effect. For moderate damping cases, I get ω0 = 12.927 ± 0.05kHz, γ = 2.067 ± 0.371 and δ = −0.33 ± 0.09. For high damping cases, results are ω0 = 13.0 ± 0.10kHz, γ = 5.001 ± 0.735 and δ = −0.33 ± 0.09.

Comparing all three phase shift fitting in Fig. 2, we can see that the phase shift drops significantly around the resonance frequency. With the damping effect, the dropping process becomes smoother. The theoretical phase shift is | − π| at f = 0, π/2 at f = ω0 and 0 at f = ∞. From the plot, the phase shift from all damping cases agrees with the theoretical value. Thus, the estimated value for ω0 stays consistent

Analysis and Result

Next, by using the length and period data, I make a fitting model. The equation for the period of a physical pendulum is $$T = 2\pi(\frac{k^2+l^2}{gl})^{0.5}$$ where k is the radius of gyration and g is gravitational acceleration constant. I use these two variables as the fitting parameters in the least-squares fitting method. With initial guess g = 9.8 and k = 0.29. The result of fitting parameters are g = 9.79 ± 0.03m/s2 and k = 0.2878 ± 0.0005m. Comparing the fitting value with data, the normalized residual appears no pattern at the first and second points. However, from the third to ninth points, there is a wave pattern in residual.

For χ2 statistic, the value is 4.68. The degree of freedom is 7. The standard deviation is 3.74. The χ2 lie within one standard deviation of the mean 68%. It shows the model is consistent with the data. The significance of getting this result is 69.8%.

From the analysis result, we can see that the significance is larger than 5%. That con- cludes the model is accepted. First, comparing the optimized k value with k value from bar’s dimension, $$k = (\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2}{12})^{0.5}$$ where a is the length, b is the width and c is the thickness of the bar. For length measurement, I can’t use a vernier caliper because it is too long, so I use a meter stick to get a value of a = 1.000 ± 0.001m. For width and thickness measurements, I use a vernier caliper which can get a more precise value. I get b = 0.03800 ± 0.00005m and b = 0.00600 ± 0.00005m. By Eqt.3, I get k = 0.2889 ± 0.0001m. The optimized value is close to the dimensional value, but it is not in the error range. So I think there are more human errors when measuring the size of the bar. Second, the accepted value ofg is equal to 9.80665m/s2[1]. The optimized value for g is g = 9.79 ± 0.03m/s2, which is in the error range. Thus, it is an acceptable value for the gravitational acceleration constant.
